Chapter 2 – Probability

What is Probability?

Probability is a branch of mathematics that deals with measuring uncertainty. It helps us determine how likely an event is to occur under given conditions. The value of probability always lies between 0 and 1. A probability of 0 represents an impossible event, whereas a probability of 1 represents a certain event. Probability is widely used in science, engineering, economics, statistics, and daily life.

  • 0 → Impossible event
  • 1 → Certain event
Click to see example & formula

If the probability of rain tomorrow is 0.7, it means there is a 70% chance of rain.

Formula: P(E) = Number of favourable outcomes / Total number of outcomes

Sample Space and Event

The sample space is the set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment. It is denoted by S. An event is any subset of the sample space and represents one or more outcomes of interest. Understanding sample space is essential for calculating probability.

Click to see example

Tossing a coin: S = {H, T} Event A (getting head) = {H}

Equally Likely Events

Events are equally likely when each outcome has the same probability of occurring. This concept applies mainly to fair experiments and is the basis of classical probability. It makes probability calculations simple and straightforward.

Click to see example

In a fair letter dice {A, B, C, D, E, F}, each letter has probability 1/6.

Complementary Events

The complementary event of A consists of all outcomes that do not belong to A. If event A occurs, its complement cannot occur. The sum of probabilities of an event and its complement is always equal to 1.

Click to see formula & example

Formula: P(A′) = 1 − P(A)

If P(A) = 0.4, then P(A′) = 0.6

Mutually Exclusive Events

Two events are mutually exclusive if they cannot occur at the same time. They have no common outcomes. This concept is important while applying the addition rule of probability.

Click to see formula & example

Formula: P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B)

Example: Getting A or F on a letter dice.

Independent Events

Events are independent if the occurrence of one does not affect the probability of the other. Independent events commonly occur in repeated experiments.

Click to see formula & example

Formula: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B)

Example: Tossing two coins.

Dependent Events

Events are dependent when the occurrence of one affects the probability of the other. This usually happens in sampling without replacement.

Click to see formula & example

Formula: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B|A)

Example: Drawing two cards from a deck without replacement.

Conditional Probability

Conditional probability is the probability of an event occurring given that another event has already occurred. It helps update probabilities using additional information.

Click to see formula & example

Formula: P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)

Example: Probability of passing an exam given that the student studied.

Bayes’ Theorem

Bayes’ theorem is used to calculate the probability of an event based on prior knowledge. It plays a vital role in statistics, machine learning, and artificial intelligence.

Click to see formula & example

Formula:
P(A|B) = [P(B|A) × P(A)] / P(B)
