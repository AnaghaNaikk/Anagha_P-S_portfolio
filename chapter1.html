Chapter 1 · Description of Data

Introduction

Descriptive statistics focuses on organizing, summarizing and presenting data in a meaningful form. It uses numerical measures and graphical methods to understand data clearly.

Types of Data

Data are classified based on their nature into qualitative and quantitative data. This classification helps decide suitable statistical methods.

Click to expand
Qualitative (Categorical) • Nominal – no order • Ordinal – ordered categories Quantitative • Discrete – countable • Continuous – measurable Examples: Nominal: Blood group Ordinal: Rank Discrete: Number of students Continuous: Height

Measures of Central Tendency

These measures describe the central or typical value of a dataset. Mean, median and mode are the most commonly used measures.

Click to expand
Mean (ungrouped): x̄ = Σx / n Median (grouped): l + [(N/2 − cf)/f] × h Mode (grouped): l + [(f₁ − f₀)/(2f₁ − f₀ − f₂)] × h Example: 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 → Mean = 30

Measures of Dispersion

Dispersion shows how far data values spread around the central value. It indicates consistency or variability in data.

Click to expand
Range = Max − Min Variance: s² = Σ(x − x̄)² / (n − 1) Standard Deviation: s = √s² Example: Data: 5, 7, 9 → SD = 2

Histogram

A histogram represents frequency distribution of continuous data using adjacent bars. The height of each bar shows frequency.

Ogive

An ogive is a cumulative frequency curve. It is used to find median, quartiles and percentiles.

Box Plot

A box plot represents data using five-number summary. It helps detect spread and outliers.
